# Business & Work — Rome

## Employment Landscape

### Overview
Rome's economy is driven by:
- Government and public sector
- Tourism and hospitality
- Professional services
- Media and entertainment
- Some manufacturing
- Growing tech (but smaller than Milan)

### Key Sectors

**Government**
- Italy's capital = massive public sector
- Ministries, agencies, international orgs
- EU and UN institutions
- Stable but bureaucratic

**Tourism**
- Hotels, restaurants, guides
- Travel agencies
- Heritage management
- Events and conferences

**Professional Services**
- Law firms (especially EU law)
- Consulting (Big 4 presence)
- Accounting
- Financial services

**Media**
- RAI (state broadcaster)
- Film industry (Cinecittà)
- Journalism
- Advertising agencies

**Academia**
- Major universities
- Research institutions
- International schools

## Employment Types

### Standard Employment (Tempo Indeterminato)
- Permanent contract
- Strong protections
- Hard for employer to terminate
- Best benefits
- Most desirable

### Fixed-Term (Tempo Determinato)
- Limited duration
- Max renewals regulated
- Less security
- Common for starting

### Internship (Stage)
- Very common in Italy
- Often unpaid or minimal pay (EUR 300-800)
- Sometimes leads to employment
- Regulated by law

### Freelance (Partita IVA)
- Self-employment
- Open VAT number
- Complex tax situation
- "Regime forfettario" for small income

### Contract Work
- Project-based
- Co.Co.Co (collaborazione)
- Less common now, regulated

## Salary & Benefits

### Salary Structure
- Gross salary quoted
- 13th month: Standard (December)
- 14th month: Common in many sectors
- Net = roughly 60-70% of gross

### Typical Salaries (Gross Annual)

| Level | Range |
|-------|-------|
| Entry-level | EUR 20-28k |
| Mid-level | EUR 30-45k |
| Senior | EUR 45-70k |
| Director | EUR 70-120k |
| Executive | EUR 100k+ |

**Notes:**
- Lower than Northern Europe
- Higher than Southern Italy
- Public sector often lower but stable
- Benefits add significant value

### Common Benefits
- TFR (severance fund)
- Health insurance (often supplemental)
- Meal vouchers (buoni pasto)
- Pension contributions
- Transport subsidies sometimes
- Flexible benefits increasing

## Work Culture

### Hours
- Standard: 40 hours/week

- Lunch: 1-2 hours traditional
- Staying late not always valued
- Work-life balance improving

### Communication

## Starting a Business

### Company Types

**SRL (Società a Responsabilità Limitata)**
- Limited liability company
- Most common for small-medium
- Min capital: EUR 10,000 (EUR 1 for simplified)
- Requires notary

**SAS/SNC**
- Partnerships
- Less common now
- Personal liability issues

**Ditta Individuale**
- Sole proprietorship
- Simplest to start
- Personal liability

**Startup Innovativa**
- Special status for innovative startups
- Tax benefits
- Simplified incorporation
- Requirements on R&D spend

### Setup Process
1. Business plan
2. Notary appointment
3. Chamber of Commerce registration
4. VAT number (Partita IVA)
5. Bank account
6. Various registrations (INPS, INAIL)

### Costs
- Notary fees: EUR 1,000-3,000
- Registration fees: EUR 200-500
- Capital requirements vary
- Commercialista (accountant): EUR 100-300/month
- Annual taxes and contributions

### Challenges
- Bureaucracy complex
- Language barriers
- Tax system complicated
- Accountant (commercialista) essential
- Slower than UK/US setup

## Freelancing (Partita IVA)

### What It Is
- VAT registration for self-employment
- Required for ongoing freelance work
- Tax and social security obligations

### Regime Forfettario
- Simplified regime for small income
- Up to EUR 85,000/year (2024)
- Flat tax rate (5% first 5 years, then 15%)
- Reduced accounting requirements
- Must meet eligibility criteria

### Costs (Forfettario)
- Tax: 5-15% of income
- INPS contributions: ~26% of taxable
- Accountant: EUR 50-150/month
- Total burden: ~40% of income

### Standard Regime
- Above forfettario limits
- Full VAT accounting
- More complex
- May need for certain activities
